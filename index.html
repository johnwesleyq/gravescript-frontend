CEO Playbook

How to Think Like a Winning Founder

This is what I'd tell you if I were your CEO for a day. No fluff. Real decisions.

🎯 Focus

Pick ONE Customer to Serve First

You have 4 segments: genealogists, cemeteries, municipalities, developers. That's 4 different products disguised as one. Pick the segment that has a BUDGET and an URGENT PROBLEM. That's developers and emergency compliance — they lose $50K/day when construction stops. Start there. Genealogists are fun. Developers pay.

💵 Revenue

The One Number That Matters: MRR

Monthly Recurring Revenue is your oxygen. Every decision maps to it. "Should I add this feature?" — Will it directly cause someone to pay or stay? If no, do it later. Your goal: $10K MRR before you spend a single dollar on advertising. That's only 67 people paying $149/month.

🛡 Moat

Your Competitive Moat Is Data, Not Code

Any developer can copy your app in 2 weeks. Nobody can copy your Graveyard Corpus — 50,000 annotated historical cemetery records — in 2 years. Every customer that signs up contributes training data (with consent). Write this into your Terms of Service on day 1. Your moat widens automatically as you grow.

  • Add "allow anonymized record use for AI improvement" checkbox to signup
  • Every 1,000 records processed = your model gets measurably better
  • Publish annual "Accuracy Report" — this becomes press-worthy
  • Patent your cemetery-specific prompt engineering methodology
⚠️ Risks to Manage

Threats That Can Kill This Business

  • Ancestry.com builds this internally — mitigate by owning the B2B compliance angle they won't touch
  • AI accuracy errors in legal documents — always show confidence scores, require human review for court use
  • Anthropic API price changes — start building towards your own fine-tuned model by month 6
  • Privacy concerns about deceased persons' data — consult a lawyer on GDPR/CCPA implications, month 1
  • You burn out building alone — find one co-founder or advisor in month 1, not month 12

From Your CEO

To Be a Better Human While Building This

"You don't have to be great to start. But you have to start to be great. Every founder who succeeded looked exactly like you do right now — uncertain, excited, a little afraid. That feeling never fully goes away. But it stops being a stop sign and starts being jet fuel."

— Your CEO Perspective

You asked me to help you succeed and be a better person in life. That's not a small ask — and it tells me you're the kind of person who will actually make this work. Here are 9 principles I'd live by if I were building this alongside you.

01

Ship ugly. Improve relentlessly.

Your first version will embarrass you. Ship it anyway. A product 100 people use is worth infinitely more than a perfect product that doesn't exist. Speed beats perfection at your stage.

02

Revenue is the only real validation.

Compliments feel good. Twitter followers feel good. Someone handing over their credit card is the only feedback that actually means your work solves a real problem. Chase that signal only.

03

Protect your energy like it's money.

Building a company is a marathon, not a sprint. Sleep 7–8 hours. Exercise three times a week. One day off per week is not laziness — it's maintenance. Burned-out founders make terrible decisions.

04

Learn to handle rejection without shrinking.

Nine of ten cold emails get ignored. Two of three pilot customers will ask for features you haven't built. This is normal. Rejection is information, not judgment. Keep a "rejection log" and find the pattern.

05

Find your people early.

Join Indie Hackers, the Anthropic developer Discord, and a local startup community. Founders who have other founders to talk to survive. The ones who build alone burn out or make preventable mistakes.

06

Give more than you take.

Answer questions in genealogy forums before you have a product. Help others. The person who helps 100 people for free builds a community that turns into 100 customers when they launch. Generosity compounds.

07

Document everything — your thinking, your failures, your wins.

Write a weekly update — even if it's just for yourself. "What did I try? What worked? What didn't? What do I believe now that I didn't believe last week?" In a year, this becomes your playbook. In five years, it's your book.

08

The mission is bigger than your product.

You're preserving human history. Every record you digitize is a story that might otherwise be lost forever. On the hard days — and there will be hard days — come back to that. You're not selling software. You're saving stories.

09

Be honest with yourself every week.

Ask: "Am I making real progress, or just staying busy?" Busy and productive look identical from the outside but feel completely different. Every Friday, ask yourself: "Did I move closer to a paying customer today?"
